Every choice answers a need

Our client can push and drag but cannot grip. Each subsystem removes one task that used to need a grip or a second person.

Design priorities

We ranked five goals and held that order through every decision.

  1. 1 Safety
  2. 2 Usability
  3. 3 Reliability
  4. 4 Portability
  5. 5 Simplicity

1. Adjustable angled wooden board

A flat table forces the child to reach across the sheet. The angled board brings the work surface toward her instead. The base is a wooden easel. The teacher sets the angle before use and locks it. Once locked, it does not move during the cut.

Why it matters: reach costs her effort and control. The angle spends neither.

2. Magnet attachment system

Cutting paper normally takes two hands, one to cut and one to hold. The magnets replace the holding hand. A galvanized steel plate sits on the board. The paper lays flat on the plate. Four neodymium magnetic strips press it down, one on each side. The board then raises and locks with the paper secured.

Why it matters: no clamp levers, no clips to pinch open. Magnets need a push to place and a pull to lift. Both motions she can do.

3. 3D printed wearable brace

Scissors need a grip she does not have. The brace removes the grip entirely. Printed at the uOttawa Makerstore, it is shaped like a U-shaped magnet closed on all sides, with an opening through the middle. Her dominant hand slides through the opening and the brace holds a Slice 10408 ceramic blade facing downward. The teacher straps it on. To cut, she drags her hand forward. There is no button and no trigger.

Why it matters: the drag is a motion she already controls well. The tool adapts to her, not the other way around.

4. Rubber feet stabilizer

Dragging the blade pushes the whole station forward. Rubber feet mounted under the board grip the table and cancel that push. The board stays where the teacher put it.

Why it matters: a station that slides mid-cut ruins the cut and shakes her confidence. This one holds still.
